# Latched — Nipple Blister Protocol & FAQ

**Status:** v1.0 draft. For advisor review.
**Last updated:** 2026-06-01
**Scope:** Blood blisters (friction/trauma) and milk blisters/blebs (plugged nipple pore). Covers all paths. Surfaced reactively when mom reports nipple pain, visible blister, or white/dark spot on nipple — not proactively pushed in the weekly module flow.
**Trigger:** Mom selects "I have a blister or sore spot on my nipple" in the symptom checker, or logs a pain note with "blister," "blood blister," "white spot," or "bleb."

## How to tell which kind you have

This distinction matters because the cause and treatment are different.

| | Blood blister | Milk blister / bleb |
|---|---|---|
| **Color** | Red, purple, or dark brown | White or yellow |
| **Location** | On the nipple surface, tip, or areola | On or just in front of a milk duct opening, usually nipple tip |
| **Cause** | Friction or trauma — wrong flange size, dry pumping, poor latch | Skin grows over a duct opening, trapping milk behind it |
| **Pain** | Sharp, immediate; worst during pumping or at latch | Deep aching or burning; often worse after feeds |
| **Common in** | Pumping moms especially; nursing moms with shallow latch | Nursing and pumping moms; can recur in same spot |

If you're not sure, treat conservatively (steps below) and reach out to your IBCLC if it doesn't resolve in 48 hours.

## MODULE: Blood Blister on the Nipple

**All paths • Reactive — surfaced when reported • Estimated read time: 3 min**

**LEAD LINE**
A blood blister on your nipple is almost always a friction injury — and the fix is usually simple once you find the source.

**WHAT TO KNOW**
A blood blister forms when friction or pressure breaks small blood vessels just under the skin. On the nipple, this almost always comes from one of three things: a pump flange that's too small and is rubbing the nipple wall, pumping without lubrication (dry friction), or a shallow latch that's compressing or twisting the nipple repeatedly.

The blister itself will heal on its own. The goal right now is to find what caused it and stop the irritation — otherwise it will come back, or get worse.

You can keep feeding and pumping through a blood blister. It may feel tender, but the blister itself does not make your milk unsafe for your baby. If the blister ruptures and bleeds during a feed, the small amount of blood is harmless to swallow — some babies will pull off if they taste it, which is fine.

**WHAT TO DO**

1. **Identify the likely cause.** If you're pumping: check your flange size (you should have roughly 2–4mm of space around your nipple inside the flange tunnel — if the nipple is rubbing the sides, the flange is too small). If you're nursing: look at the latch — is your nipple coming out misshapen (pointy or lipstick-shaped) after feeds?

2. **Add lubricant before every pump session.** Apply a small amount of nipple butter, lanolin, coconut oil, or olive oil directly to the nipple before placing the flange. This reduces friction dramatically and is often all that's needed to prevent recurrence. This should become standard practice, not just a fix for when things go wrong.

3. **Protect the blister.** After feeds or pump sessions, apply a thin layer of medical-grade lanolin (Lansinoh, Medela Tender Care) or nipple butter to the area and let it air dry briefly before covering. Hydrogel pads (cooled in the fridge) can soothe pain between sessions.

4. **Do not drain or pop it.** A blood blister that opens on its own can heal; one that's punctured intentionally has a much higher infection risk. Leave it alone.

5. **Check and replace your pump parts.** Cracked or worn membranes, valves, and flanges cause irregular suction and friction. If it's been more than 2–3 months since you replaced membranes and valves, replace them now.

**WHAT TO WATCH FOR — when to reach out**
- The blister is not improving after 48–72 hours of the steps above
- The area around the blister becomes red, warm, or swollen beyond the blister itself
- You develop fever or flu-like symptoms (this would suggest infection progressing toward mastitis — call your OB today)
- The blister opens and the skin underneath looks infected (yellow discharge, increasing pain, spreading redness)
- Pain is severe enough that you're considering stopping — reach out to your IBCLC first; there may be a fixable cause

**[CRASH-COURSE SNIPPET — expandable]**
*"Want to go deeper? — Flange fitting and pump lubrication"*

Flanges (also called breast shields) come in a range of sizes — typically 17mm to 36mm — and most pumps ship with 24mm as a default. Most people are not 24mm. Sizing yourself correctly takes about 2 minutes:

1. Measure your nipple diameter at the base (not the areola) in millimeters, ideally after a warm shower when the tissue is relaxed.
2. Add 2–4mm to that measurement. That's your starting flange size.
3. During pumping, look at how your nipple moves in the tunnel: it should move freely without rubbing the sides, and a small amount of areola may be drawn in — but the nipple should not be pulling in so much tissue that it feels painful or cuts off milk flow.

A correct flange eliminates the most common cause of blood blisters in pumping moms.

On lubrication: anything food-safe and emollient works — lanolin, coconut oil, olive oil, nipple butter. Apply to the nipple before the session. It will not contaminate your milk.

## MODULE: Milk Blister / Bleb

**All paths • Reactive — surfaced when reported • Estimated read time: 3 min**

**LEAD LINE**
A milk blister (sometimes called a bleb) is a small plug of skin that's grown over a milk duct opening — it looks like a white or yellow dot on the nipple tip and can be surprisingly painful.

**WHAT TO KNOW**
A milk blister forms when a thin layer of skin grows over the opening of a milk duct, trapping milk behind it. The pressure of backed-up milk is what causes the pain — a deep burning or aching sensation, often worst at the end of a feed when the breast should be feeling relieved.

Blebs can recur in the same spot, especially in moms prone to blocked ducts. They're more common with oversupply, in pumping moms (particularly if flange fit is off), and in moms who've had mastitis.

Like blood blisters, you can continue feeding and pumping. In fact, nursing from the affected side is often the most effective treatment — the suction can open the blocked pore and release the trapped milk.

**WHAT TO DO**

1. **Apply moist heat before every feed or pump session.** Soak a washcloth in warm water and hold it against the nipple for 3–5 minutes before nursing or pumping. A warm shower works too. Softening the skin makes the bleb easier to open.

2. **Nurse or pump immediately after the heat.** The suction often breaks the bleb open naturally. You may feel or see a small release of thick or stringy milk — that's the trapped plug releasing. It should bring relief quickly.

3. **Gentle friction if needed.** After the warm soak, gently rub the spot with a clean, wet washcloth. Do not dig at it with your fingernail or any sharp object.

4. **Olive oil soaks.** Some moms find that soaking the nipple in a small cup of warm olive oil for 10–15 minutes before feeding softens the skin over the bleb and speeds resolution.

5. **If the bleb doesn't open on its own after 48 hours** of the above, contact your IBCLC. Some IBCLCs can open a persistent bleb with a sterile needle under appropriate conditions — this is not something to attempt at home with a regular needle.

6. **After the bleb opens:** keep the area clean and apply lanolin after feeds to help the skin heal. Watch for signs of infection (increasing pain, redness, swelling, fever).

7. **If blebs recur frequently:** talk to your IBCLC or OB. Recurrent blebs can sometimes be reduced by lecithin supplementation (sunflower or soy; typically 1,200mg 3–4x/day) — this is not a cure but reduces milk stickiness and may decrease frequency. Discuss with your provider before starting.

**WHAT TO WATCH FOR — when to reach out**
- The bleb hasn't opened after 48–72 hours of moist heat + nursing/pumping
- The area is developing signs of infection: spreading redness, swelling, warmth, discharge
- You develop a fever or flu-like symptoms (this can indicate mastitis — call your OB today)
- You're experiencing pain severe enough to affect feeding frequency — reach out to your IBCLC before reducing feeds, as skipping feeds can worsen a bleb or cause a blocked duct

**[CRASH-COURSE SNIPPET — expandable]**
*"Want to go deeper? — Why blebs happen and how to reduce recurrence"*

Blebs are more common in moms with:
- Oversupply (more pressure in the ducts)
- A history of blocked ducts or mastitis (scar tissue can create irregularities)
- Frequent changes in feeding frequency (supply-demand mismatches)
- Tight or poorly fitting bras and tops (external pressure on ducts)

The lecithin approach: lecithin is a phospholipid that's naturally present in breastmilk. Taking a supplement may reduce milk viscosity (make it "slipperier"), which can reduce the frequency of blebs and blocked ducts in moms prone to them. Sunflower lecithin is preferred over soy lecithin for moms with soy sensitivities. This is the most evidence-adjacent approach available; it's not a sure thing but has a strong safety profile and is commonly recommended by IBCLCs.

Diet and clothing: some moms notice that tight clothing or underwire bras correlate with recurrence. There is limited clinical evidence for this, but if you're prone to recurrent blebs, it's worth tracking whether there's a pattern.

## FAQ — Nipple Blisters

**Q: I popped my blood blister. What do I do now?**
Clean the area gently with saline solution or a clean cloth with warm water. Apply a thin layer of lanolin or antibiotic ointment (Bacitracin is commonly used) and cover loosely with a non-adherent dressing or a hydrogel pad if you have one. Watch for signs of infection over the next 24–48 hours: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, or discharge beyond a small amount of clear fluid. If you see any of those, contact your OB.

**Q: My blister bled during a feed. Is my milk safe?**
Yes. A small amount of blood in breastmilk is not harmful to your baby. Some babies pull off the breast when they taste it — that's fine, just switch sides and try again at the next feed. If you're pumping and see blood in the bottle, you can still give that milk to your baby; if it bothers you, you can discard that session's output.

**Q: Can I still pump and nurse with a blister?**
Yes. You don't need to take a break from feeding or pumping. In fact, continuing to feed is usually part of the treatment, especially for blebs. The goal is to address the cause (friction, latch, flange fit) so you're not making it worse, not to rest the nipple entirely.

**Q: How do I know if my flange size is causing this?**
The clearest sign: your nipple is rubbing against the side of the flange tunnel during pumping. You might notice this as visible friction, a red mark on the side of the nipple after pumping, or pain that's worst during the pumping session itself rather than just at latch-on. If the nipple has any white marks, compressed areas, or blistering on the sides (rather than the tip), that's friction from a flange that's too small.

**Q: I've been using lubricant and my blister still came back. What now?**
If you've corrected your flange size, you're lubricating before every session, and blood blisters are still recurring, something else may be driving it: worn pump parts causing irregular suction, an inefficient pump, or a latch issue if you're nursing. Contact your IBCLC — this is diagnosable and fixable, but it needs a live evaluation.

**Q: The bleb keeps coming back in the same spot. Is there something wrong with me?**
No. Recurrent blebs in the same location are common and usually mean there's a small structural irregularity at that duct opening. This is manageable — lecithin supplementation, staying well-hydrated, and regular feeding frequency all help reduce recurrence. Your IBCLC may also have additional strategies based on your specific situation.

**Q: How long does a blood blister take to heal?**
With the cause corrected (flange size fixed, lubrication in place, latch improved), most blood blisters heal within 3–7 days. If it's not improving after a week, something is still irritating the area.

**Q: How long does a milk blister take to resolve?**
Most blebs that open with moist heat and nursing resolve within 1–3 days. Stubborn ones that don't open with home treatment may need an IBCLC to help release them. After opening, the skin heals in another few days.

**Q: Do I need antibiotics?**
Not for a blister by itself. Antibiotics are for infection. A blister becomes infected if the skin breaks and bacteria enter — the signs are increasing redness, warmth, swelling, fever, or discharge that looks purulent (cloudy, yellow-green). An uncomplicated blister, even one that has opened, does not require antibiotics.

**Q: I have a fever and my breast hurts. Is this from the blister?**
Fever plus breast pain is mastitis until proven otherwise — and mastitis needs to be addressed promptly. Call your OB today. A blister can be an entry point for infection, but mastitis often develops independently. Either way, the protocol is the same: contact your provider, continue feeding from the affected side (it's safe and helps), and expect to be evaluated for antibiotics.
