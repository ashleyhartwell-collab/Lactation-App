Title: How to Hand Express

---

LEAD LINE

Hand expression is worth learning even if you plan to pump — in the first days, your hands collect colostrum better than any pump can.

---

WHAT TO KNOW THIS WEEK

Section label: WHAT TO KNOW

Paragraph 1:
Before your mature milk arrives around days 3-5, your body produces colostrum — thick, golden, and concentrated. Colostrum doesn't move through pump tubing easily. It sticks to flanges, tubing walls, and collection bottles before it ever reaches the container. Your hands bypass all of that. By pressing and releasing directly at the breast, you can collect colostrum more completely than most pumps can in these first days.

Paragraph 2:
Hand expression also triggers letdown through skin contact in a way pumping doesn't replicate as well. That matters in the early hours when your body is still learning the signals.

Paragraph 3:
The technique is useful beyond just those first days. You can use hand expression to soften an engorged breast so your baby can latch more easily, to finish emptying after a pump session, or to get some relief when your pump isn't nearby.

Paragraph 4:
Give yourself a few tries. The first time feels awkward and produces very little. That's normal. Most moms find the rhythm within one or two sessions.

STEP 4

Title: Compress and release

Description:
Bring your thumb and fingers toward each other in a rolling motion — rolling forward toward the nipple — without sliding your fingers on the skin. Then release and repeat. The rhythm is: press back, roll forward, release. Press back, roll forward, release. Aim for about one compression per second.

Title: Catch what comes out

Description:
Hold a 1-3mL syringe (available at most pharmacies) close to the nipple to catch drops as they appear. On days 1-2 you may see just a few drops — that is completely normal. Collect it. Every drop of colostrum in the first days is concentrated and valuable.

WHAT TO DO

Section label: WHAT TO DO

1. Get a 1-3mL syringe from a pharmacy before your baby is born, or ask for one at the hospital. You will want it in the first 24-48 hours.

2. Start with warmth. Thirty seconds of a warm washcloth on the breast makes a noticeable difference, especially when you're learning.

3. If you're getting nothing after a few minutes, check your finger placement. The most common mistake is placing fingers too close to the nipple. Move back a little and try again.

4. Do this alongside your pump in the first 72 hours — not instead of pumping, since the pump still provides important stimulation. Hand express first, then pump.

---

WHAT TO WATCH FOR

Section label: WHAT TO WATCH FOR

Block 1 — Amber/warning accent (IBCLC or care provider):
Pain during hand expression that is sharp or located in the nipple itself usually means your finger placement is too close to the nipple, or your fingers are sliding on the skin instead of rolling. Adjust your position before the next session. If pain continues with correct technique, mention it to your IBCLC.

Block 2 — Plain text (non-escalation):
No drops appearing in the first session is common, especially on day 1. Keep the session short (5-10 minutes), rest, and try again after the next feed or with a warm compress. Most moms see something by day 2.

Block 3 — Amber/warning accent (IBCLC or care provider):
If you have been hand expressing and/or pumping 8 or more times per day and are seeing truly zero output — no drops at all after day 2 — contact your IBCLC or OB.

CRASH-COURSE SNIPPET

Trigger label: "Want to go deeper?"
Section title inside: "Why your hands outperform the pump for colostrum"

Content:
Colostrum is substantially more viscous than mature milk. That thickness is part of what makes it so nutrient-dense, but it's also why pumps struggle with it. Most breast pumps are optimized for the flow properties of mature milk — thinner, higher-volume, moves readily through tubing with suction. Colostrum doesn't behave the same way. A meaningful percentage gets lost in the flanges, the tubing, and the collection bottle walls before it reaches the collection container.

Hand expression bypasses the mechanical path entirely. Gentle compression at the breast moves colostrum directly to the nipple with no tubing, no suction loss, and no dead volume to fill. The Academy of Breastfeeding Medicine (ABM Clinical Protocol #8) and the American Academy of Pediatrics both support hand expression as the preferred method for colostrum collection in the first 24-72 hours for exactly this reason.

Hand expression also involves direct skin contact, which supports oxytocin release in a way that pumping alone doesn't replicate as effectively in the early days. Oxytocin is the hormone that drives letdown — the physical release of milk — so anything that supports its release improves collection efficiency.

Colostrum storage quick reference:

Room temperature (up to 77°F): 4 hours
Refrigerator (39°F or below): 4 days
Freezer (0°F standard): 6 months
Deep freeze (-4°F): 12 months

Use the smallest container that holds what you've collected. Colostrum stored in a large bag is colostrum wasted on the walls.
