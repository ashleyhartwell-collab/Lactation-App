LEAD LINE

Most of what you're feeling this week is your body shifting into milk-making mode, and understanding what's happening makes it a lot easier to work with.

---

WHAT TO KNOW THIS WEEK

When your baby was born, estrogen and progesterone dropped sharply. That drop is what signals your body to start producing milk at full volume. Prolactin, the hormone that builds your milk supply, starts climbing. Oxytocin, which triggers letdown, also starts causing your uterus to contract. If you feel strong cramping in your lower abdomen when your milk lets down, that's why. It's uncomfortable, and it's also your body shrinking your uterus back toward its normal size and managing postpartum bleeding. For moms who have given birth before, afterpains can be intense. They typically peak around days 2-3 and ease from there.

Around days 3-5, your breasts will change noticeably. Colostrum transitions to mature milk, and when it arrives, your breasts may become fuller, heavier, and harder than you expected. This is engorgement. It's normal, it's temporary, and the best response is to feed frequently so the milk keeps moving. If your baby is having trouble latching because the breast feels too firm, try pressing gently around the areola for 30-60 seconds before the latch. This is called reverse pressure softening, and it makes the tissue easier for your baby to grasp.

Your body is also shedding the extra fluid it held during pregnancy. Night sweats are common in the first one to two weeks and usually peak around week 2. Keep water nearby wherever you feed.

Here's a quick map of what to expect in the weeks ahead. By the end of week 2, postpartum bleeding will lighten considerably and uterine cramping during feeds will ease. Around weeks 3-4, many moms notice their breasts feel softer as supply settles into a rhythm matched to their baby's intake. That's not a supply drop. It's your body getting smarter. The crash-course snippet below has a full week-by-week breakdown.

WHAT TO DO

1. If afterpains during nursing are intense, ibuprofen taken about 30 minutes before a feed can help significantly. Confirm with your provider that it's cleared for you.

2. When engorgement arrives around days 3-5, feed more often, not less. If your baby can't latch on a very firm breast, apply gentle pressure around the areola for 30-60 seconds to soften it first.

3. Keep a water bottle next to wherever you typically feed. You're making milk and recovering from birth at the same time, and thirst is easy to ignore when you're tired.

4. Expect night sweats in the first couple of weeks. This is your body clearing out the extra fluid from pregnancy. It's temporary.

---

WHAT TO WATCH FOR

Block 1 — Red accent (ER or provider same-day):
Fever above 100.4°F combined with breast pain and flu-like symptoms is mastitis, not normal engorgement. Call your OB or provider today.

Block 2 — Red accent (ER or provider same-day):
Postpartum bleeding that soaks through a pad in under an hour for two or more hours in a row, or bleeding that passes large clots, warrants a call to your provider.

Block 3 — Amber/warning accent (IBCLC or pediatrician):
Engorgement severe enough that you cannot get your baby to latch and hand expression is not providing relief: contact your IBCLC. Engorgement rarely needs intervention, but when it does, earlier is better.

Block 4 — Amber/warning accent (IBCLC or pediatrician):
Nipple pain that is sharp or that continues throughout the full feed, not just at latch-on, is a latch issue, not something your body has to adjust to. Contact your IBCLC.

Content (prose, same style as WHAT TO KNOW):

Week 1. Estrogen and progesterone fall sharply after birth, triggering the shift from colostrum to mature milk production around days 3-5. Your uterus, which is roughly the size of a grapefruit right after birth, contracts back toward its normal size using oxytocin — the same hormone that drives letdown. Postpartum bleeding is heavy and bright red. Breast engorgement arrives with the milk. Afterpains during nursing are common and often stronger than first-time moms expect.

Week 2. Postpartum bleeding (lochia) shifts from bright red to pink or brown and lightens considerably. Uterine cramping during nursing becomes much milder. Night sweats often peak this week as your body sheds pregnancy fluid. Breast size may begin to stabilize.

Weeks 3-4. Uterine recovery is largely complete. Lochia is usually very light or done. Your milk supply transitions from being driven by the postpartum hormone flood to being driven by how often your baby feeds. This is when many moms notice their breasts feel softer between feeds. That's not a supply problem — it's supply calibrating to what your baby actually needs. Hair shedding often begins around weeks 8-12 for the same hormonal reasons as milk production. It's temporary and unrelated to nutrition or feeding method.

Weeks 5-6. Most physical recovery from birth is well underway. Perineal healing or C-section recovery should be significantly improved. For moms who are not exclusively nursing, a period may return. For those who are, it often stays away for months. Breast tissue may look and feel quite different from the early postpartum weeks — less obviously full, more like it did before pregnancy. Your supply is still there; it just doesn't announce itself the way it did at week 1.
