# Latched — V1 Protocol Content Outline (All 3 Paths)

**Status:** v1.0 draft. For advisor review.
**Last updated:** 2026-05-24
**Scope:** Weeks 1-6 across three paths: (A) Exclusive Nursing, (B) Exclusive Pumping, (C) Combination Feeding. Weeks 7-12 "Continue Your Journey" extension deferred to v1.1; a minimal 3-module preview will be offered at the week-6 graduation screen.
**Format:** Each week = 2-3 modules. Each module = one main topic, mobile-readable, ≤300 words of protocol text + embedded crash-course snippet (the "deep dive" if she wants more). This is not a textbook. Shorter is harder to write and more important to get right.

---

## Module structure

Every module follows the same skeleton:

```
MODULE TITLE
Week X • Path [A|B|C|all] • Estimated read time: 2-3 min

LEAD LINE
One sentence. What this module is about and why it matters right now.

WHAT TO KNOW THIS WEEK
2-4 short paragraphs. What's happening in her body and her baby. Normal vs. concerning.
Written in 2nd person, plain language, no jargon without definition.

WHAT TO DO
2-4 specific, actionable things. Not "stay hydrated" — "drink a glass of water every time you sit down to feed."

WHAT TO WATCH FOR (when to reach out)
Specific signals that mean she needs a human: IBCLC, pediatrician, or OB.
Never buried. Always at the bottom of the module. Never optional.

[CRASH-COURSE SNIPPET — expandable]
Title: "Want to go deeper?"
More detail, diagrams, linked resources. The mom who wants to learn more taps here.
The exhausted mom at 3am does not have to.
```

## Path A — Exclusive Nursing (18 modules across weeks 1-6)

### Week 1 — Foundation (3 modules)

**Module A-1-1: Your First Feeds — What's Actually Happening**
- Colostrum is milk. Debunk the "my milk hasn't come in" myth proactively.
- What colostrum looks like (thick, yellow to clear), why small volume is normal
- Skin-to-skin: why it works, how to do it, what "responsive feeding" means
- Feeding frequency normal range: 8-12 times per day (including nights)
- The diaper count rule of thumb: 1 wet diaper per day of age in the first week
- Crash-course snippet: the biology of milk production (demand-supply system explained simply)

**Module A-1-2: Getting the Latch**
- What a good latch looks and feels like (asymmetric, wide-mouth, no clicking)
- What a shallow latch feels like (nipple pain that persists past initial latch, lipstick-shape nipple after feeding)
- Common latch positions: cradle, cross-cradle, football hold, laid-back nursing
- The one fix to try first: re-latch instead of suffering through
- When nipple pain is normal (first 10-15 seconds on latch) vs. when it isn't (pain throughout feed)
- Crash-course snippet: illustrated latch positions with labeled photos

**Module A-1-3: The First Week Survival Guide**
- Cluster feeding: what it is, why it's happening (not a supply problem), how long it lasts
- Engorgement at day 3-5 when milk transitions from colostrum: normal, what to do (feed frequently, reverse pressure softening before latch, cold compress after)
- Sleep setup: safe feeding in a chair vs. bed; partner-feeding strategy
- What to eat and drink (evidence is thin; debunk the "you must eat 500 extra calories exactly" myth; keep it simple: eat real food, drink to thirst)
- When to call your IBCLC: baby losing >10% of birth weight by day 5, fewer than expected wet diapers, every feed is painful
- Crash-course snippet: the first 72 hours timeline

**Module A-2-1: Your Milk Is Here — Now What?**
- Milk transition from colostrum to transitional to mature milk: what it looks like, what to expect

**Module A-2-3: Is My Baby Getting Enough?**

- Crash-course snippet: counting diapers in weeks 2-4

### Week 3 — Common Complications (3 modules)

**Module A-3-1: The Supply Myth**
- Why most moms who think they have low supply don't
- The three real causes of genuinely low supply (insufficient glandular tissue, hormonal factors, not feeding/pumping frequently enough)
- What actually doesn't increase supply: the evidence on oats, fenugreek, lactation cookies (honest; don't oversell)
- What does affect supply: feed frequency, breast emptying, hydration and stress
- Crash-course snippet: galactagogues — what the evidence actually says

**Module A-3-2: Blocked Ducts and Mastitis — Know the Difference**
- Blocked duct: what it feels like, what to do (keep feeding/pumping from that side, heat before, gentle massage during, cold after — NOT aggressive massage)
- When a blocked duct becomes mastitis: fever + flu symptoms + localized breast pain
- Mastitis action: continue feeding, seek antibiotics promptly, self-resolving cases are rare
- **Escalation prominent:** if you have fever above 101°F + breast pain + flu symptoms, call your OB or provider today — do not wait
- Crash-course snippet: the anatomy of a blocked duct

**Module A-3-3: Pumping for the First Time (Even If You're Not a Pumper)**
- Why nursing moms may need to pump: engorgement relief, building a small stash before returning to work, offering a bottle so partner can feed
- When to introduce a bottle: typically weeks 3-6 — early enough that baby accepts it; not so early that latch is still being established (paced bottle feeding applies here too)
- How to pump without undermining supply: pump after a feed, not instead of one, in the early weeks
- Crash-course snippet: paced bottle feeding — why it matters even for nursing moms

## Path B — Exclusive Pumping (21 modules across weeks 1-6)

*Note: exclusive pumping is one of the most underserved categories in lactation support. Most IBCLC training is latch-focused. This path needs more modules than exclusive nursing because the logistics are more complex and the emotional experience is distinct.*

### Week 1 — Starting Your Pumping Journey (4 modules)

**Module B-1-1: Why You're Pumping and Why That Matters**
- The decision to exclusively pump is valid, full stop. No need to justify or apologize.
- Why exclusive pumping works: the body doesn't know the difference between a baby and a pump — it responds to demand
- What makes EP harder than nursing: more time (pump + bottle + wash), more equipment, no skin-to-skin hormonal feedback loop
- What makes EP potentially easier: others can help with feeds, you can measure output, more flexibility for some moms
- Crash-course snippet: the 4 hormones of milk production and how pumping triggers them

**Module B-1-2: Your Pump Setup**
- Hospital-grade vs. consumer double electric vs. wearable: which to use when
- First-week recommendation: hospital-grade pump (rental) if available; this is the critical window for supply establishment and wearables are less efficient than hospital-grade
- Flange fitting — do this now, not later: a wrong flange size will damage your nipples and reduce output within days. Link to the flange sizing tool.
- Pump settings: start low and slow, find the vacuum level that's effective without pain, cycle speed settings
- Cleaning: what's actually required vs. overkill; the "quick clean in fridge between pumps in first few days" caveat for immunocompromised situations
- Crash-course snippet: labeled pump diagram with each part's function

**Module B-1-3: Your Pumping Schedule — Week 1**
- Goal: 8-10 pumping sessions per day including overnight. This is not negotiable in week 1 for supply establishment.
- Duration: pump until milk stops flowing, then 2-5 more minutes. Typically 15-20 min per session.
- Overnight: at least one session between midnight and 5am. Prolactin peaks 2-4am.
- The hardest truth: skipping sessions in week 1 has a disproportionate impact on total supply. Front-load the effort.
- Crash-course snippet: how to schedule 8-10 sessions around sleep (sample 24-hr schedule)

**Module B-1-4: What to Expect From Your Output in Week 1**
- Day 1-2: colostrum only; drops, not ounces. This is normal.
- Day 2-5: transitional milk comes in; output increases. Still not full volume.
- Day 5-14: milk matures; most EP moms start to see more predictable output
- What "normal" EP output looks like in week 1: 15-30 mL total per session in the early days scaling up
- Storing colostrum: syringe, small bottle; don't store in large containers until volume justifies it
- **Escalation:** if you are having zero output after 72 hours of pumping 8x/day, contact your IBCLC or OB — rare but warrants attention
- Crash-course snippet: EP output norms by week (chart; weeks 1-6)

### Week 2 — Establishing Your Supply (3 modules)

**Module B-2-1: Supply Is Established in the First 6 Weeks — Here's Why That Matters Now**
- The prolactin receptor theory: the milk-producing cells being stimulated in weeks 1-6 determine your long-term supply capacity. This is the investment window.
- What "establishing supply" actually means: enough stimulation and emptying to tell your body how much milk to make going forward
- Pump emptying: how to tell if you're emptying well (output drops at the end of a session; breasts feel softer)
- Crash-course snippet: the biology of supply establishment for EP moms

**Module B-2-2: Output Expectations and the Comparison Trap**
- "Normal" EP output at week 2: most moms produce 20-35 oz/day by week 2 (varies widely — this is a range, not a target)
- The comparison trap: EP Facebook groups and Instagram will show you moms with 60+ oz freezer stashes. This is a skewed sample. Supply matches baby's needs, not the size of your freezer.
- How to know if YOUR output is adequate: baby is gaining weight and having wet diapers
- When to investigate output: dropping without explanation, consistently less than baby's intake needs

**Module B-2-3: Bottle Feeding Your EP Milk**
- Paced bottle feeding: how and why (prevents bottle preference, slows intake to match breastfed feeding pace)
- How much to offer per bottle: newborn stomach size by week, typical intake norms
- Bottle preparation: warming (optional), safe handling of expressed milk
- Crash-course snippet: paced bottle feeding technique with video reference (Global Health Media)

### Week 3 — Pumping Logistics at Scale (4 modules)

**Module B-3-1: Cleaning Without Losing Your Mind**
- What FDA/CDC actually says about pump cleaning (vs. what the manual says vs. what the EP groups say)
- The "refrigerator method" and its legitimate use cases
- Microwave steam bags: do they work? Are they safe?
- Cutting cleaning time: practical strategies for a mom doing 8+ pump sessions/day
- Crash-course snippet: pump part replacement schedule (flanges, valves, membranes, tubing — most moms replace way too late)

**Module B-3-2: Building a Freezer Stash (If You Want One)**
- Why a stash: buffer for illness, schedule disruption, return to work
- How much to build: one day's worth of feeds is a good starting target; more is not always better (fresh milk has more of some immunological properties)
- Milk storage containers: bags vs. bottles, first-in-first-out logistics
- Freezer storage times: 6 months standard, 12 months deep freeze
- Milk donation: HMBANA if you overproduce significantly

**Module B-3-3: Flange Fitting Follow-Up**
- Re-check flange fit at week 3: nipples can change as supply establishes
- Signs your flange is the wrong size: nipple rubbing sides of the tunnel, more areola being pulled in than nipple, output lower than expected, pain beyond initial latch
- Crash-course snippet: how to measure and what the measurement means for major pump brands

**Module B-3-4: The EP Mental Load**
- Honest acknowledgment: EP is relentless in a way that nursing isn't — there is no "just put her to the breast." Every feed is a 3-part process.
- Community: the EP community (r/ExclusivelyPumping, Exclusive Pumping by Stacey Stewart) is uniquely supportive
- Permission structure: if you need to drop to 7 sessions this week because you're running on zero — that's a decision you're allowed to make. The protocol will tell you how to do it without tanking supply.
- When to contact your IBCLC: persistent pain at the pump, output dropping without explanation, considering stopping

### Week 4 — Optimizing and Sustaining (3 modules)

**Module B-4-1: When to Start Wearables**
- Hospital-grade or consumer double electric is the recommendation through week 4 (or until supply is well-established)
- Wearables (Elvie, Willow, Momcozy) as *supplement* to, not replacement for, a conventional pump in early weeks
- Wearable pump comparison: what each handles well and where each falls short clinically
- How to add a wearable session: use it for the mid-day sessions where output is naturally lower; keep the conventional pump for morning sessions
- Crash-course snippet: wearable pump settings guide for Elvie and Willow (most common issues)

**Module B-4-2: Power Pumping — What It Is, When to Use It**
- What power pumping is: mimicking cluster feeding to signal demand increase
- Standard protocol: 20 min on, 10 off, 10 on, 10 off, 10 on = 60 min total, 1x/day for 3-7 days
- When it works: temporary supply dip, returning to work supply drop, deliberate supply increase before a disruption
- When it doesn't work: it's not magic; underlying supply issues (hormonal, glandular) don't respond to more pumping
- Crash-course snippet: EP supply toolkit (power pumping, hands-on pumping technique, massage)

**Module B-4-3: Returning to Work Preview**
- If return is weeks 6-10: workplace pumping rights under the PUMP Act (2023)
- Logistics: portable cooler, pump bag that doesn't look like a pump bag, how to ask for a private space
- Stash target before returning: 3-5 days of feeds as a buffer
- Crash-course snippet: return-to-work EP schedule templates

### Week 5 — The Long Game (3 modules)

**Module B-5-1: Is Your Supply Where You Want It?**
- By week 5 most EP moms have a clear sense of their supply ceiling
- How to know if you've hit your ceiling: output has been stable for 2-3 weeks despite consistent schedule
- If your supply exceeds baby's needs: oversupply isn't always a gift — clogged ducts, high-fat-low-foremilk imbalance, milk waste
- If your supply is below baby's needs: combination feeding with formula is a valid choice, not a failure

**Module B-5-2: Combination Feeding Transition (If Needed)**
- If you're considering adding formula: this module doesn't push you toward or away from it
- How to add formula without stopping pumping (route to combo path if she selects it)
- How to gradually reduce pumping if transitioning to weaning
- Crash-course snippet: understanding your own feeding goals — no goal is too small

**Module B-5-3: Nipple and Breast Health Check**
- White spots, blebs: what they are, how to treat, when to escalate
- Vasospasm: the "rainbow nipple" phenomenon; usually Raynaud's-related; treatable
- Persistent output decline with breast pain: could be blocked duct progressing; do not ignore
- Crash-course snippet: breast and nipple health visual guide

## Path C — Combination Feeding: Nursing + Pumping + Formula (21 modules across weeks 1-6)

*Note: this path is the most emotionally complex. The framing must be explicitly non-judgmental. Combination feeding is a choice, not a fallback. The guilt module is not optional — it goes in week 1.*

### Week 1 — Setting Up Combination Feeding (4 modules)

**Module C-1-1: What Combination Feeding Is (And Isn't)**
- Definition: any mix of nursing, pumped milk, and/or formula is combination feeding
- This path is chosen intentionally for a range of reasons — none of which need to be justified
- Common reasons: supplementing for medical reasons (weight, supply, tongue tie), personal choice, sharing feeding with a partner, reducing nursing pain while supply establishes, returning to work
- The one thing this path requires above all others: paced bottle feeding (previewed here, detailed in C-1-3)
- Crash-course snippet: the spectrum of combination feeding — from "mostly nursing with occasional formula" to "mostly formula with occasional nursing"

**Module C-1-2: Your Combination Feeding Plan**
- Why having a plan (even a flexible one) matters: it prevents reactive decisions at 3am when you're exhausted
- A plan has 4 components: primary feeding method, when to supplement, how much to supplement, who gives supplemental feeds
- Types of combination plans: nurse first / top-up with formula; designated bottle feeds; pumped milk as primary / nurse when convenient; formula as primary / nurse for comfort
- Crash-course snippet: how to decide when to offer a bottle vs. nursing first

**Module C-1-3: Paced Bottle Feeding — Non-Negotiable on This Path**
- Why this matters: a fast-flow bottle will make your baby prefer the bottle over the breast in as little as 2-4 days (the "flow preference" issue)
- Paced bottle feeding technique: baby semi-upright, horizontal bottle, let baby control the pace with pauses every few sucks, feed should take 15-20 minutes
- Teat/nipple choice: slowest flow nipple available; change only when baby is showing frustration, not on a schedule
- This applies to both pumped milk and formula
- Crash-course snippet: paced bottle feeding video reference (Global Health Media "Cup and Spoon Feeding" and "Responsive Bottle Feeding")

**Module C-1-4: Formula Basics**
- Formula is a safe, regulated, complete food. This is a statement of fact, not reassurance for reassurance's sake.
- How to choose: all standard US infant formulas meet the same FDA nutritional requirements; marketing differences are largely cosmetic; ask your pediatrician if there's a specific reason to choose otherwise (dairy intolerance, etc.)
- Safe preparation: powder vs. ready-to-feed vs. concentrate; water safety; temperature; never microwave
- Safe storage: prepared formula in the fridge (use within 24 hrs), opened powder (use within 1 month)
- Crash-course snippet: formula prep step by step (with photos)

### Week 2 — Protecting Your Supply While Supplementing (3 modules)

**Module C-2-1: How Supplementation Affects Your Supply**
- The supply-and-demand reality: every formula feed that replaces a nursing or pumping session sends a "less demand" signal. This isn't a judgment — it's information to help you manage intentionally.
- If you want to maintain your nursing supply: replace nursing sessions with pumping sessions when formula is given so the signal to your body stays consistent
- If you're okay with your supply naturally decreasing: that's a valid choice; here's how to manage the transition gradually

**Module C-2-2: The Guilt Module**
- Calling it what it is: feeding guilt is real, common, and not earned
- The cultural messaging that makes formula feel like failure is a cultural problem, not a clinical one
- Your goal is a fed, loved baby and a functional, healthy parent. Neither formula nor nursing makes you a better or worse parent.
- When to take the feeding guilt seriously as a PPD/PPA signal: if the guilt is consuming you, constant, or spiraling — that's worth talking to someone about. Link to PPD resources.
- Crash-course snippet: the evidence on formula outcomes (honest, not minimizing: well-nourished formula-fed babies thrive; breastfed babies have some population-level benefits; these are statistics, not your individual baby's destiny)

**Module C-2-3: Tracking Combination Feeding**
- The tracker for combination feeding moms: how to log both feeds and bottles
- Why tracking matters more on this path: you're managing a more complex system; the data helps you see patterns
- Simple daily intake math: how many oz total your baby likely needs by week (general ranges; always follow pediatrician's guidance for your specific baby)

### Week 3 — Finding Your Rhythm (3 modules)

**Module C-3-1: Your Personal Combination Feeding Rhythm**
- Most combination-feeding moms find their rhythm by week 3: the proportion of nursing vs. pumped vs. formula settles
- It doesn't have to be the same every day
- Signs your current rhythm is working: baby is gaining weight, having adequate diapers, you are not exhausted to the point of inability to function (mildly exhausted is normal)
- Signs to adjust: baby seems unsatisfied consistently after feeds, nipple pain at every feed, you dread every feeding moment

**Module C-3-2: Nursing on the Combo Path**
- If you're nursing as part of your combination: same latch guidance as Path A
- Nursing when supplementing with formula: supply will calibrate to how often you nurse
- Nursing for comfort vs. nutrition: both are valid; you don't have to track every nursing session as a "feed" if it's primarily comfort
- Crash-course snippet: keeping nursing pleasurable when you're also pumping and formula feeding

**Module C-3-3: Adjusting the Formula-to-Milk Ratio**
- How to intentionally shift the ratio if your goals change (more nursing vs. less, more formula vs. less)
- Increasing nursing: replace a formula feed with a nursing or pumping session; takes 2-3 days for supply to respond
- Increasing formula: add a formula top-up after nursing rather than replacing a feed; let supply naturally decrease if desired
- Crash-course snippet: the combo feeding flexibility diagram
