## Category Notes

Nutrition is not a formal category in the v1.1 launch taxonomy (it was deferred alongside galactagogues, which were removed from Category 10). These entries are drafted as a pre-launch addition to address one of the highest-anxiety topic areas for postpartum moms.

This category splits naturally across two concerns:
1. **Maternal nutrition and wellbeing** — what she actually needs, what matters and what doesn't
2. **Galactagogue claims and lactation product marketing** — what the evidence actually shows for products moms are already buying

The tone for this category needs to be especially careful: moms are receiving enormous commercial pressure about "eating for milk supply" and many have spent real money on lactation products. The goal is to be honest without being dismissive, and to redirect toward what actually works (milk removal frequency) without making moms feel foolish.

**Evidence note:** ABM Protocol #9 (galactagogues) is the primary clinical reference for entries NUT-009 through NUT-011. For dietary guidance, the CDC Maternal Diet page and the 2020-2025 Dietary Guidelines for Americans (Part D, Chapter 3 — Lactation) are the authoritative sources.

id: NUT-001
category: Nutrition & Hydration
paths: A, B, C
weeks: all (peak query volume: weeks 1–4)
question_variants:
  How many extra calories do I need while breastfeeding?
  Do I need to eat more calories to make milk?
  How much more food should I eat while nursing or pumping?
  What's the caloric need for a breastfeeding mom?

answer:
  Breastfeeding uses real energy — most moms need about 330–400 extra calories per day compared
  to before pregnancy. That's roughly the equivalent of a small meal or two substantial snacks.
  Your body is remarkably efficient at making milk even when your diet isn't perfect, so this
  isn't a strict daily prescription so much as a reminder: skipping meals and running on empty
  isn't sustainable, and eating very little (under about 1,500 calories/day) can affect your
  supply over time. Eat when you're hungry, focus on whole foods when you can, and don't stress
  about being precise with the number.

id: NUT-002
category: Nutrition & Hydration
paths: A, B, C
weeks: all
question_variants:
  Does my diet have to be perfect for my milk to be nutritious?
  If I eat junk food will my milk be bad for my baby?
  Does what I eat affect the quality of my breast milk?
  Will a poor diet affect my baby's nutrition through breastmilk?

answer:
  For the most part, your body protects your baby first. When your diet is low in something, it
  pulls from your own stores to maintain milk composition — so your milk stays nutritious even
  on harder eating days. That said, a few nutrients genuinely depend on what you eat: DHA
  (omega-3), vitamin D, iodine, B12, and choline in your milk do reflect your intake. The
  bigger picture is that eating well matters for you — your energy, recovery, and mood. A varied,
  reasonably balanced diet most of the time is the realistic goal. No mom eats perfectly in the
  newborn weeks, and your baby's milk is better protected than most people realize.

escalation_trigger: no

sources:
  CDC — Maternal Diet and Breastfeeding. cdc.gov
  Shahavand et al. (2024) — Maternal Lifestyle Factors Affecting Breast Milk Composition and
  Infant Health: A Systematic Review. Nutrients, MDPI. mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/1/62
  Human Milk Composition: Nutrients and Bioactive Factors. PMC3586783.

advisor_reviewed: pending
review_due: 2027-06
confidence_tier: high
notes:
  Macronutrient content of breast milk (protein, fat, total calories) is relatively stable
  regardless of maternal diet. Micronutrient content — especially vitamins D, B12, iodine, and
  DHA — is more diet-dependent. The reassurance here is well-supported but the nuance about
  specific micronutrients is important context for the advisor to confirm framing.

id: NUT-003
category: Nutrition & Hydration
paths: A, B, C
weeks: all
question_variants:
  How much water should I drink while breastfeeding?
  Am I drinking enough water while nursing?
  How do I stay hydrated when breastfeeding and pumping?
  What's the hydration recommendation for nursing moms?

answer:
  The general guideline for breastfeeding moms is about 13 cups (roughly 3 liters) of total
  fluids per day — but the most practical measure is thirst. Breastfeeding naturally makes you
  thirstier, which is your body doing its job. Keeping a water bottle nearby during feeds or
  pump sessions and drinking a glass whenever you nurse or pump covers most of it without
  counting ounces. Signs you're running low: dark urine, headaches, fatigue, or a noticeably
  dry mouth. You don't need to force extra fluid beyond what thirst tells you — drinking more
  than you need does not increase milk production.

escalation_trigger: no

sources:
  CDC — Maternal Diet and Breastfeeding. cdc.gov
  PMC11243499 — Investigating Water Balance as a Nutritional Determinant in Breastfeeding (2024).
  Dietary Guidelines for Americans 2020-2025, Part D, Chapter 3.

advisor_reviewed: pending
review_due: 2027-06
confidence_tier: high
notes:
  The 13-cup figure is the Dietary Guidelines recommendation for breastfeeding women and includes
  fluids from food sources, not just beverages. Advisor may want to clarify whether to state it
  as total fluids or just beverages to avoid confusion.

id: NUT-004
category: Nutrition & Hydration
paths: A, B, C
weeks: all
question_variants:
  Will drinking more water make more milk?
  Does extra water boost milk supply?
  I'm drinking so much water to help my supply — is that actually helping?
  Does drinking more water increase breast milk production?

answer:
  More water does not mean more milk — and this one surprises a lot of moms. Milk volume is
  driven by how often and how thoroughly milk is removed, not by how much you drink. Staying
  adequately hydrated matters (significant dehydration can affect supply), but drinking beyond
  your natural thirst adds nothing to output. A Cochrane review found no benefit to increased
  fluid intake on milk production. If you're chronically thirsty and feeling depleted, checking
  hydration is worthwhile — but forcing yourself to chug extra water past the point of thirst
  won't move the needle. The core lever is nursing or pumping frequency.

escalation_trigger: no

sources:
  Cochrane review — Extra Fluids for Breastfeeding Mothers: no benefit found for supply.
  PMC11243499 — Investigating Water Balance as a Nutritional Determinant in Breastfeeding (2024).

advisor_reviewed: pending
review_due: 2027-06
confidence_tier: high
notes:
  This entry addresses a persistent myth that circulates heavily on social media. The advice to
  "drink a gallon a day" to increase supply causes real distress when moms can't keep up and
  still have supply concerns. Honest debunking is warranted and well-supported by the Cochrane
  evidence. Pair with NUT-003 for the full hydration picture.

  store-brand prenatals is a real gap during lactation.

  Why is iodine important while nursing?

  150 mcg for non-pregnant adults. Your baby depends on iodine in your milk for thyroid function

  reach this through food alone, especially if they avoid seafood or iodized salt. It's also one
  of the most commonly missing nutrients in prenatal vitamins. Food sources include seafood,
  dairy, eggs, and iodized salt. If your prenatal doesn't contain at least 150 mcg of iodine,
  it's worth discussing a supplement or intentional food sources with your provider.

id: NUT-007
category: Nutrition & Hydration
paths: A, B, C
weeks: 1–4
question_variants:
  Does my baby need vitamin D drops if I'm breastfeeding?
  My doctor said to give vitamin D drops — is that really necessary?
  Can I take extra vitamin D myself instead of giving baby drops?
  What do I need to know about vitamin D and breastfeeding?

answer:
  Breast milk is generally low in vitamin D regardless of your own status — and the AAP
  recommends all exclusively breastfed infants receive 400 IU of vitamin D daily as a supplement,
  starting within the first few days of life. It's the one supplement that's standard practice
  for breastfed newborns, so your pediatrician is right to recommend it. If giving drops to a
  baby sounds like one more thing, there is evidence that high-dose maternal supplementation
  (around 6,400 IU/day) can raise milk vitamin D enough to meet your baby's needs — but this
  requires your provider's guidance since it's a significant dose. The infant drops are the
  simpler, well-established path.

id: NUT-009
category: Nutrition & Hydration
paths: A, B, C
weeks: all
question_variants:
  Do lactation cookies actually increase my milk supply?
  Are lactation cookies worth the money?
  I bought lactation cookies — will they help my supply?
  Do lactation cookies and bars work?

answer:
  The honest answer: the research doesn't support the marketing. A 2023 randomized controlled
  trial in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition directly tested lactation cookies containing
  oats, brewer's yeast, flaxseed, and fenugreek against regular cookies — and found no difference
  in milk production between the groups. Neither produced more milk. Most lactation cookies are
  also calorie-dense, which isn't a problem if you're genuinely under-eating, but the ingredients
  themselves don't appear to drive supply. The more reliable lever is nursing or pumping frequency.
  If you enjoy them, they're not harmful — just know what you're paying for.

  Are teas like Mother's Milk or MoreMilk tea worth trying?

  Lactation teas typically contain fenugreek, blessed thistle, fennel, or other herbs believed
  to support milk supply. The evidence for most of them is thin — limited trials, small samples,

  can cause GI symptoms and a maple-syrup smell in urine and sweat. Staying hydrated genuinely
  matters for breastfeeding, so if a warm tea makes you drink more and feel grounded in a
  difficult stretch, there's real value in that. Just be thoughtful with any product combining
  multiple herbs, especially if you're on other medications, and check with your provider if
  you're unsure.

id: NUT-011
category: Nutrition & Hydration
paths: A, B, C
weeks: all
question_variants:
  Do oats, brewer's yeast, or fenugreek increase milk supply?
  Are galactagogue foods worth eating while breastfeeding?
  Does fenugreek actually help with milk supply?
  Should I eat oatmeal every day to make more milk?

answer:
  Most of these have a reputation bigger than the evidence behind them. Fenugreek is the most
  studied galactagogue and results are genuinely inconsistent — some moms notice a difference,
  most studies show no reliable benefit over placebo, and it can cause GI upset and a
  maple-syrup odor. Brewer's yeast has even less clinical support. Oats are the most useful of
  the three, though not primarily because of a galactagogue effect — they're a good source of
  iron and provide sustained energy, which matters a lot for a postpartum body running short on
  sleep. Eating oatmeal is a worthwhile habit regardless of whether it moves supply. And for any
  galactagogue food: none of it works reliably if milk isn't being removed frequently enough.

id: NUT-012
category: Nutrition & Hydration

question_variants:
  How much caffeine is safe while breastfeeding?
  Can I drink coffee while nursing or pumping?
  Is caffeine bad for my baby through breast milk?
  How many cups of coffee can I have while breastfeeding?

answer:
  Up to about 200–300 mg of caffeine per day — roughly 2 cups of regular coffee — is considered
  safe during breastfeeding by both the CDC and ACOG. Only a small percentage of caffeine passes
  into breast milk, and it peaks about 1–2 hours after you drink it. Most babies handle this
  without any issue. Newborns metabolize caffeine more slowly than older babies, so some moms
  notice signs of sensitivity in the early weeks — jitteriness, trouble settling to sleep, or
  unusual fussiness. If that's happening, cutting back is worth trying. If it's not, there's no
  clinical reason to give up your morning coffee.

id: NUT-013

  Can I have a glass of wine or beer while breastfeeding?
  Is it safe to drink alcohol while nursing or pumping?

  Occasional, moderate alcohol during breastfeeding is considered low-risk by the AAP and CDC —
  but timing matters. After one drink, wait at least two hours before nursing or pumping, since
  alcohol peaks in breast milk within 30–60 minutes of consumption and then clears as your blood
  level drops. A practical guideline: if you feel sober enough to drive, your milk is likely clear.
  Small amounts of alcohol can disrupt infant sleep patterns and temporarily inhibit letdown, so
  routine heavy drinking is a different situation and worth discussing with your provider. One
  occasional drink is not the same as regular consumption.

id: NUT-014
category: Nutrition & Hydration
paths: A, B, C
weeks: all
question_variants:
  Does pumping and dumping remove alcohol from breast milk faster?
  Should I pump and dump after drinking alcohol?
  Will expressing and throwing away my milk clear the alcohol sooner?
  Does pumping help get rid of alcohol in my milk?

answer:
  Pumping and dumping does not speed up alcohol clearance — this is one of the most common
  misconceptions about alcohol and breastfeeding. Alcohol moves freely between your blood and
  your milk, which means it leaves your milk at exactly the same rate it leaves your bloodstream.
  Expressing milk after a drink removes milk that already contains alcohol, but the milk your
  body makes next will contain alcohol at the same concentration until enough time has passed.
  The only thing that clears alcohol is time. If you need to pump for comfort while waiting,
  that's fine — just discard that milk if you're within the two-hour window.

escalation_trigger: no

sources:
  CDC — Alcohol and Breastfeeding.
  cdc.gov/breastfeeding-special-circumstances/hcp/vaccine-medication-drugs/alcohol.html
  AAP — Alcohol & Breast Milk. healthychildren.org

advisor_reviewed: pending
review_due: 2027-06
confidence_tier: high
notes:
  Very common myth with a clear clinical answer. Warrants its own entry separate from NUT-013
  because many moms have specifically heard that pump-and-dump removes alcohol faster and are
  acting on that belief. The answer here is direct and factual without being alarming.

  Do I need to avoid certain foods to prevent gas or fussiness in my baby?
  Does garlic, broccoli, or dairy in my diet make baby gassy?
  Should I cut foods out of my diet while breastfeeding?
  Does what I eat cause my baby to have tummy troubles?

answer:
  For most moms, there are no foods that need to be eliminated while breastfeeding — and the
  long list of "foods to avoid" that circulates online is largely not evidence-based. Gas-causing

  percentage of babies (estimated 3–5%) do have a sensitivity to cow's milk protein in maternal
  diet, which can appear as excessive fussiness, blood in stool, or a rash. That's worth
  discussing with your pediatrician — but it's not something to assume without a clear pattern.

  you suspect a dietary connection. If you notice a clear and consistent pattern of fussiness,
  rash, or digestive distress after specific foods, discuss an elimination trial with your
  provider before removing entire food groups.

id: NUT-020
category: Nutrition & Hydration
paths: A, B, C
weeks: all
question_variants:
  How does alcohol actually get into breast milk?
  Is the alcohol level in my milk the same as in my blood?
  How much alcohol actually reaches my baby through my milk?
  Does alcohol filter differently into breast milk than other things I drink?

answer:
  Alcohol doesn't need to be filtered into breast milk — it moves there passively, and your
  milk alcohol level tracks your blood alcohol concentration almost exactly. They rise together,
  peak together, and fall together. The amount that ends up reaching your baby is small: research
  estimates roughly 5–6% of your weight-adjusted alcohol dose transfers into milk. That said,
  newborns metabolize alcohol much more slowly than adults do, so even a small dose stays in
  their system longer. The clinical term for this transfer rate (Relative Infant Dose, or RID)
  is estimated at around 16% for alcohol — which places it in an intermediate zone: not
  negligible, not alarming with occasional use, but real enough that timing genuinely matters.
  Understanding that milk tracks blood is the key insight: you cannot filter alcohol out of
  milk, but you can wait for your body to clear it from both at once.

id: NUT-024
category: Nutrition & Hydration

answer:
  This surprises most moms: alcohol doesn't help letdown — it impairs it. Alcohol inhibits
  oxytocin release in the brain, and oxytocin is the hormone that triggers the milk ejection
  reflex. Research found this effect is dose-dependent: even at moderate amounts, drinking
  delays letdown and reduces the volume of milk babies actually receive during a feed. Babies
  fed by moms who drank before nursing tend to take in less milk in that session and compensate
  by nursing more frequently in the 8–16 hours afterward. The belief that "a beer helps you
  relax and letdown" is persistent, and psychological ease does matter — but the oxytocin
  data runs in the opposite direction at the physiological level. The practical implication:
  drinking right before nursing means your baby gets less milk than usual and gets some alcohol
  in what they do get. Neither outcome is the goal.
