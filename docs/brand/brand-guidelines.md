## 1. Brand Foundation

### 1.1 Mission ✅

> To support, encourage, and educate women during the biggest change in their lives: becoming a mother and establishing breastfeeding.

### 1.2 Vision *(🔲 Needs Detail)*
> *Where is this brand going in 5 years? What does success look like for the families who use this app?*

### 1.3 Brand Tenets ✅

These are the principles that guide every product, content, and design decision. When in doubt, come back here.

1. **Transparency is at the core of everything we do.** We are honest about what we know, what we don't know, and where our information comes from.

2. **Knowledge sharing is a top priority — no gatekeeping.** Information that helps a mother feed her baby should never be locked behind a paywall, jargon, or a specialist's gatekeeping. We share it freely and clearly.

3. **We are not a replacement for a physician, and we don't pretend to be.** We inform and support; we do not diagnose, treat, or advise in place of qualified medical care. We always point users back to their care team when appropriate.

4. **Every woman deserves a fair chance at breastfeeding, if she so chooses.** We remove barriers — informational, emotional, and practical — so that a woman's feeding journey is shaped by her own choice, not by a lack of support.

5. **Breastfeeding isn't easy, but it shouldn't be intimidating — or reserved for any one type of mother.** This is for every mom, not just those who already know what they're doing. We make it feel doable.

6. **The journey belongs to her — all of it.** We support the path, whatever it looks like — not just the breastfeeding parts.

## 2. Target Customer ✅ (Partially Established)

**Primary Audience:** First-time mothers in the first 6 weeks postpartum (the "fourth trimester").

### Who She Is
- Navigating breastfeeding for the first time, often with no prior experience
- Likely sleep-deprived and emotionally vulnerable
- Information-seeking but easily overwhelmed — needs answers fast
- May feel pressure from family, culture, or healthcare providers around feeding choices
- Probably on her phone one-handed, often in the middle of the night

### What She Feels
Anxious, hopeful, exhausted, and desperate for reassurance that she's doing it right.

### What She Needs From Us
- Validation before instruction
- Confidence-building language, not clinical detachment
- Fast, scannable answers (she doesn't have time to read paragraphs)
- A sense that the app understands her specific moment, not just "breastfeeding" in general

## 3. Tone of Voice ✅ (Partially Established)

### Core Descriptors
| Attribute | What It Means | What It Doesn't Mean |
|-----------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Warm** | We feel like a knowledgeable friend, not a textbook | Overly casual, dismissive of clinical realities |
| **Informative** | We lead with what she needs to know | Jargon-heavy or data-dumping |
| **Straightforward** | We get to the point; we don't hedge unnecessarily | Blunt or cold; skipping emotional acknowledgment |

### Voice Principles
1. **Lead with empathy, follow with information.** Acknowledge the moment before diving into the answer.
2. **Say it plainly.** If a simpler word exists, use it. Prefer "latch" over "neonatal oral motor attachment."
3. **Be confident, not prescriptive.** Offer guidance; respect her autonomy.
4. **Never shame.** No language that implies a "right" way. Every mother's journey is valid.
5. **Be concise.** She's tired. Respect her attention.

### Specific Tone-of-Voice Rules ✅

These apply to all in-app copy, FAQ answers, push notifications, and any content where the brand speaks directly to the user.

**1. No absolute or definitive language about her body or her baby.**
Avoid words like "always," "never," and definitive clinical labels like "you have low milk production." Breastfeeding is highly individual. Absolutes close doors; this brand keeps them open.
- ❌ "You'll always feel tenderness in the first week."
- ✅ "Tenderness in the first week is common for many moms."
- ❌ "You have low milk supply."
- ✅ "There are a few signs worth looking at together."

**2. No curse words.** Full stop. The user may be exhausted and emotional, but we stay measured. The brand's warmth comes from clarity and care, not casual language.

**3. Don't validate or agree with negative self-talk.** If a user expresses that she's failing, she's bad at this, or she's not good enough — don't mirror that language back to her, even in a sympathetic framing. Acknowledging the feeling is different from validating the framing.
- ❌ "I understand this feels like a failure."
- ❌ "It makes sense that you feel like you're not doing enough."
- ✅ "Breastfeeding challenges are rarely about personal effort — and they're almost never about what you're doing wrong."
- ✅ "Your worth as a parent has nothing to do with ounces."

The right move: redirect toward facts, toward agency, toward what she can do next — without echoing the negative framing she brought to the question.

**4. Keep it short.** Standard FAQ answers: 400–600 characters. Complex clinical questions (tongue tie, pumping troubleshooting, supply issues with medical context): 900 characters max. No exceptions without a documented reason. Gut check: can a one-handed, sleep-deprived mom scan this in under 10 seconds?

**5. Write like a human, not a content system.** Every response should pass a "ghost" test — it should not read like it was written by AI. Vary sentence structure. Don't start consecutive sentences the same way. Avoid formulaic openings like "Great question" or "It's important to note." Read it out loud. If it sounds stiff or robotic, rewrite it.

**6. Use em-dashes sparingly.** No more than one per response, and only when a comma or period genuinely won't work. Overuse of em-dashes is one of the strongest signals of AI-generated writing.

### Tone-of-Voice in Action: FAQ Examples ✅
*(Source: Latched — Smart-FAQ Library, Google Drive. Rewritten to reflect brand tone.)*

---

**Q: How do I know if I have enough milk?**

The most reliable signs are in your baby — not in how your breasts feel or how much you pump.

Look for: 6 or more heavy wet diapers a day (starting around day 5), at least 3 bowel movements per day in the first month, steady weight gain (back to birth weight by 2 weeks, then 5–7 oz per week), and a baby who seems satisfied after most feeds and has wakeful, alert periods.

Soft breasts after feeds are a sign of normal regulation, not a sign of low supply. If you want a precise check, a weighted feed with an IBCLC — weighing your baby before and after a full nursing session — is the most accurate way to see exactly how much transferred.

---

**Q: I feel like a failure because breastfeeding isn't going the way I planned.**

Breastfeeding challenges are rarely about personal effort — and they're almost never about what you're doing wrong. More often they come from gaps in early support, latch issues that take time to identify, or supply factors that have biological roots outside your control.

Your worth as a parent has nothing to do with ounces. Whatever feeding method keeps you and your baby nourished and connected is the right one.

If you want to keep working through the challenges, a certified lactation consultant (IBCLC) can often change the picture significantly — earlier support tends to make a real difference. If you're weighing a different feeding path, that's also a choice you're allowed to make.

If these feelings are persistent or feel heavier than you'd expect, it's worth talking to your provider. Postpartum anxiety and depression are common and very treatable.

---

**Q: How do I know if my baby has a good latch?**

Figuring out a good latch is one of the first things you and your baby are learning together — and it can take a few days to find your rhythm.

Here's what to look for: baby's mouth wide open, taking in not just the nipple but a generous mouthful of areola — more areola showing below the nipple than above. Baby's chin should touch the breast, with their nose clear or just grazing. You'll hear rhythmic swallowing and see jaw movement. It should feel like firm pulling pressure — not pinching or sharp pain. After a feed, your nipple should come out round, not flat or lipstick-shaped.

Some tenderness right at the start of a feed is common in the early weeks as your skin adjusts. If pain continues throughout the entire feed, that's usually a signal the latch is worth revisiting.

---

**Q: Is it okay to stop breastfeeding and switch to formula?**

Yes — and you don't owe anyone an explanation.

Formula is a safe, complete nutrition source for babies. There are many reasons a mom might choose to transition — physical recovery, supply challenges, emotional wellbeing, practical demands, or simply feeling like it's time — and any of them counts.

If you're able to wean gradually (reducing one feeding at a time over a couple of weeks), that tends to be easier on your body. It allows your supply to wind down without engorgement. If you need to stop more quickly, hand-expressing just enough for comfort — not to fully empty — can ease the transition.

This is your journey. What matters is that you and your baby are nourished and taken care of.

---

**Q: My supply seems to have dropped suddenly — why?**

A sudden drop usually has an identifiable cause, which means it's often fixable once you know what you're looking for.

Most common reasons: worn pump valves or membranes (check these first — this is the culprit more often than people expect), fewer feeds or sessions than usual, starting hormonal birth control (especially any estrogen-containing method), a returning menstrual period (a temporary dip around ovulation is common), stress or illness, or returning to work and pumping less frequently than baby was nursing.

Check your pump parts first. Valves and membranes lose their seal over time and are inexpensive to replace. If parts look fine, think about whether anything hormonal or logistical has changed in the last few days.

Increasing the frequency of nursing or pumping tends to help in most situations, though it generally takes a few days to see a response. If you've looked at the common causes and the dip continues, a lactation consultant can help identify what's happening.

## 5. Imagery & Visual Style ✅ (Partially Established)

### Three Approved Styles

#### 5.1 Infographics
- Use for: step-by-step content (latch positions, pumping schedules), statistics, how-to guides
- Style: clean lines, minimal illustration, consistent icon set, brand-color fills
- Avoid: cluttered layouts, clip-art aesthetics, overly complex diagrams

#### 5.2 Isometric & 3D Visuals
- Use for: feature illustrations, onboarding screens, marketing/social content
- Style: soft shadows, rounded forms, warm palette integration, not photorealistic
- Avoid: cold tech aesthetics, overly complex scenes, anything that competes with photography

#### 5.3 Photojournalistic & Authentic Photography
- Use for: hero moments, editorial content, emotional storytelling
- Style: natural light, diverse representation (body type, ethnicity, family structure), candid over posed
- Subjects: real moments — feeding, skin-to-skin, exhausted-but-loving, partners participating
- Avoid: stock photo aesthetics, overly perfect/filtered imagery, all-white or homogeneous representation

### 5.4 Rules for Mixing Styles ✅

**What "mixing styles" means, in plain terms:**
The three visual styles above are like three different looks in your brand's wardrobe — infographics are the clean diagram, isometric 3D is the polished illustration, and photography is the real moment. "Rules for mixing" just means: when you put more than one of these looks in the same place (same screen, same post, same piece of content), here's how to do it without it looking messy or like three different apps.

**The rules:**

1. **One dominant style per screen.** Every screen in the app should have a clear visual "lead." If you're using a photo, that's the lead. An infographic overlay can sit on top of it. But you shouldn't have a photo, an isometric illustration, and an infographic diagram all competing on the same screen — pick one to run the show.

2. **Photography + infographic overlay is the one combination that works well together.** This is actually your brand's "signature mix." A real photo carries the emotional weight; a clean infographic callout delivers the specific information. Think: a photo of a mom nursing with a simple visual diagram indicating latch positioning overlaid on it. This combination is approved and encouraged.

3. **Never put isometric/3D illustration directly next to a real photograph.** Illustrated 3D objects and real people live in different visual worlds — placing them side-by-side makes the brand feel disjointed. Keep them on separate screens or separate pieces of content. (Exception: isometric imagery used as a background behind text, not directly paired with a photo, can work.)

4. **On social media: one style per post, variety across the feed.** A single Instagram post or story should live cleanly in one visual style. Your feed as a whole can — and should — contain all three styles for visual interest, but don't mix styles within a single post or carousel slide.

5. **When in doubt, lead with photography, support with infographic.** If you're ever unsure which style to use, photography is the emotional anchor and infographic is the information layer. This pairing is your safest default for any content that needs to do both jobs — connect and inform.

All colors below are drawn from **[Radix Colors](https://www.radix-ui.com/colors)** (MIT license) and **[Open Color](https://yeun.github.io/open-color/)** (MIT license). Both are free for commercial use, have strong accessibility documentation, and include built-in dark-mode pairings.

> **Before finalizing:** Validate all combinations with a contrast checker such as [Accessible Colors](https://accessible-colors.com) or [Coolors Contrast Checker](https://coolors.co/contrast-checker). Both are free.

### Direction
- **Avoid:** sterile clinical whites/blues (hospital vibes) and oversaturated "baby pink" (feels infantilizing or dated)
- **Approach:** Warm neutrals as the canvas; muted teal-sage as the primary trust color; terracotta/clay as a warm accent
- **Healthcare note:** Color conveys calm and confidence. Reserve red/orange for genuine alerts only — never for decorative use.

## 7. Accessibility Standards ✅

> For a healthcare app serving sleep-deprived, often one-handed users at 3am, accessibility is a brand value, not a checklist item. Many of these standards also apply to users with disabilities — this is both the right thing to do and a legal consideration.

### Compliance Target
**WCAG 2.1 AA** is the mandatory minimum for all UI. Aim for **WCAG 2.1 AAA** for any screen containing health information or instructions.

Free resources: [WebAIM WCAG Checklist](https://webaim.org/standards/wcag/checklist) · [A11y Project Checklist](https://www.a11yproject.com/checklist/)

---

### Visual Standards

| Standard | Requirement | Why It Matters Here |
|----------|------------|---------------------|
| **Color contrast (body text)** | Minimum 4.5:1 ratio | Users in dim rooms, tired eyes, OLED screens |
| **Color contrast (large text / headings)** | Minimum 3:1 ratio | Text ≥18pt regular or ≥14pt bold |
| **Color contrast (UI components)** | Minimum 3:1 ratio | Buttons, inputs, icons must be distinguishable |
| **Color alone** | Never the sole indicator | Error states need color + icon + text label |
| **Focus indicators** | Visible on all interactive elements | Required for keyboard/switch access users |

### Typography Standards

| Standard | Requirement |
|----------|------------|
| **Minimum body text size** | 16px / 1rem — never smaller for paragraph content |
| **Line height** | Minimum 1.5× font size for body text |
| **Dynamic Type (iOS)** | Must support all sizes — never lock font sizes in-app |
| **Font Scale (Android)** | Must respect system font scale setting |

> ⚠️ Blocking the OS text-size setting is a common accessibility failure in health apps. If a user has set large text in System Settings, the app must honor it.

### Interaction Standards

| Standard | Requirement |
|----------|------------|
| **Touch target size** | Minimum 44×44pt (Apple HIG) / 48×48dp (Material Design) |
| **One-handed use** | Primary actions reachable in the bottom 2/3 of the screen |
| **Tap spacing** | Minimum 8px between adjacent touch targets |
| **Session timeouts** | Warn users before auto-logout; give option to extend |
| **Motion** | Respect "Reduce Motion" system setting; no autoplay animations |

### Screen Reader Support
- All images require descriptive `alt` text (or marked decorative)
- All icons used as buttons require accessible labels
- Reading order in layouts must match visual order
- Form inputs require programmatic labels (not just placeholder text)
- Health content screens should be fully navigable via VoiceOver (iOS) and TalkBack (Android)

### Healthcare-Specific Additions
- **Error messages on health inputs** must never rely on color alone — always pair with an icon and plain-language description
- **One-handed operation** is not optional — consider bottom-sheet navigation, large tap targets, and thumb-zone-aware layout
- **Night mode / dim display** — test all color combinations at low brightness; some users will read in completely dark rooms
- **Reading level** — target Grade 6–8 for all in-app content (use [Hemingway App](https://hemingwayapp.com) — free — to check)

## 8. Brand Naming & Language ✅ (Partially Established)

### How We Address the User
Users choose how they want to be referred to during onboarding. Options presented include:
- Their first name
- Their nickname
- Mama
- Mom
- Mommy

> **Implementation note:** Store the preference and use it consistently throughout the app — in push notifications, headers, empty states, and all personalized copy. Never default to a generic term once a preference has been set.

> **UX principle:** Asking this question during onboarding is itself a brand moment. It signals immediately that *this app sees you as an individual*. The copy around this prompt should be warm and unhurried — not a checkbox.

### How We Address the Baby
We refer to the baby by their name or nickname, not generically as "your baby" or "baby." This establishes an emotional connection and reflects that the family is real and specific, not a use case.

- Ask for baby's name (and nickname, if they have one) during onboarding
- Use the baby's name in personalized content, milestone moments, and prompts
- If no name is provided yet (baby is brand new), default to "your little one" — never "the baby" or "it"

> 💡 **This is a meaningful differentiator.** Most health apps refer to the baby generically. Using a name makes the app feel like it actually knows this family.

### Language Style Rules

| ✅ Use | ❌ Avoid |
|--------|---------|
| Her first name / chosen name | "Mom" as a generic label once preference is set |
| Baby's name | "Your baby," "the baby," "it" |
| Plain language (latch, nipple, milk) | Clinical jargon without explanation |
| "If you choose to" / "if that's right for you" | Prescriptive phrasing ("you should," "you must") |
| "It's normal to feel..." | "You shouldn't worry about..." |
| Short sentences | Paragraphs over 3–4 lines |
