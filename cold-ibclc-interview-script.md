# Cold IBCLC Validation Interview Script

**Goal:** Validate whether hospital and outpatient IBCLCs will recommend a paid commercial app to their patients, what conditions enable or block that, and what they actually need from a tool like Latched. This determines whether the B2B2C distribution thesis holds.

**Why this matters:** Your friend IBCLCs will be supportive because they're friends. Strangers won't. If 3+ of 5 strangers hedge or decline, the channel thesis is weaker than v0.5 assumes and you'd want to know now — not after the IBCLC dashboard is built.

---

## Recruiting

**Target: 5 IBCLCs who are NOT your friends or friends-of-friends.** Mix of settings:

- 2-3 hospital-based IBCLCs (ideally from different hospital systems)
- 1-2 outpatient or private-practice IBCLCs
- 1 IBCLC who works in WIC, public health, or community settings

**Where to find them:**
- USLCA (US Lactation Consultant Association) member directory
- ILCA (International Lactation Consultant Association)
- Cold LinkedIn outreach (search "IBCLC" + city)
- Texas Lactation Consultant Association (relevant given BSWH context)
- La Leche League Leader contacts (often have IBCLC referrals)
- Ask your friend IBCLCs for 1 introduction each (1-degree-removed counts as cold for this purpose)

**Outreach script (use LinkedIn or email):**

> Subject: Quick research — 30 min on IBCLC workflow
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm researching the post-discharge gap between hospital lactation visits and outpatient follow-up. I'd love 30 minutes of your time to learn how IBCLCs actually handle this in practice — what works, what's broken, what tools would help. This isn't a pitch, and I won't try to sell you anything.
>
> I'll pay $75 for your time as a thank-you (Amazon gift card, Venmo, or donation to a charity of your choice).
>
> Available: [3 specific time windows]
>
> Thanks,
> Ashley

**Notes on incentive:** $75 is appropriate for a 30-min professional interview. Cheaper than market rate ($150-200 IBCLC hourly), enough to signal you're serious, not so much that it's a conflict.

## Interview structure (~30 min)

Run it on Zoom or phone. Record only with verbal consent at the start. Take notes by hand (more useful than transcripts for this).

### Warm-up (3 min)

The point here is to build rapport and get them talking comfortably. Don't extract data; just listen.

1. "Tell me a little about your work — how long have you been an IBCLC, what setting are you in, and what does a typical week look like?"

### Their work and patient flow (5 min)

Goal: understand the actual workflow so you know where Latched would slot in.

2. "Walk me through what happens with a typical first-time mom who's trying to breastfeed — from when you first meet her to when you see her last."
3. "How long do you have with each patient on average — both in the room and across the whole relationship?"
4. "What's the moment in that journey when you feel most useful to a mom?"

### What happens after discharge — the meat (10 min)

This is the heart of the interview. Latched's distribution thesis depends on what they say here. Go slow, ask follow-ups, don't pitch.

5. "After a mom is discharged from your care, what happens next from your end? Do you stay in touch, or is it a hand-off?"
6. "Do you ever find out how she did? If yes, how do you find out? If no, does that bother you?"
7. "Tell me about a recent patient where you wished you knew what happened — what was the situation? What did you wish you could have done?"
8. "When you think about your patients calling the hospital lactation line at 3am with breastfeeding questions — does that happen? What do they call about most? Who answers?"
9. "What's the gap between when you see them and when they see their pediatrician or outpatient IBCLC? What happens in that gap?"

### What they recommend today (5 min)

Goal: understand current behavior around recommending external resources — the closest existing behavior to what Latched needs them to do.

10. "When a mom asks you what to read or what to do at home, what do you actually tell her? Do you have a printed handout? Do you recommend specific apps, books, or websites?"
11. "Have you ever recommended a paid app or service to a patient? Which one, and how did you feel about doing that?"
12. "Does your hospital or practice have any policy about recommending paid commercial products to patients? Have you ever been told to stop recommending something, or warned about conflicts of interest?"
13. "If you do recommend something paid, what makes you trust it enough to recommend? What's the bar?"

### Concept reaction — ONLY AT THE END (5 min)

Don't introduce the concept until here. Earlier and you contaminate every previous answer.

14. "I'm going to describe a product idea and I want your honest gut reaction — no need to be polite. It's an app for new moms in their first 6 weeks postpartum. It picks a path with the mom — exclusive nursing, exclusive pumping, or combo — and gives her a personalized 6-week protocol. It includes a flange-sizing tool, basic feed and pump tracking, and 24/7 AI chat support reviewed by IBCLCs. It costs the mom $49 one-time. What's your honest first reaction?"
15. "Would you recommend something like that to your discharged patients? Why or why not?"
16. "What would make you start recommending it? What would make you stop?"
17. "If you did recommend it, how would you actually do that — verbally? Discharge packet? Card? Some other way?"

### Wrap-up (2 min)

18. "Is there anyone else doing what you do who you think I should talk to?"
19. "Anything I didn't ask about that I should have?"

Pay them. Send the gift card or Venmo before the call ends so they don't have to chase you.

## What to listen for

**Green flags (channel works):**
- They already recommend specific apps/services and feel fine about it
- Hospital policy is "use your judgment" or non-existent
- They light up when talking about losing patients post-discharge
- They volunteer specific moms they wish they could have helped
- They ask "when can I see it?" or "can I have your card?" without you prompting

**Yellow flags (channel works with friction):**
- They recommend free resources only
- Hospital policy is "no paid recommendations" but in practice it's not enforced
- They'd recommend it but only if they could vet it first themselves
- They want a way to recommend without explicitly endorsing (a printed list of options)

**Red flags (channel is in trouble):**
- They've been told explicitly not to recommend commercial products
- They see recommending a paid app as a conflict with their own practice
- They are visibly uncomfortable with the $49 paywall ("moms shouldn't have to pay")
- They can't think of any moms they'd recommend it to
- They want to know how they get paid for referring (this is a different business model entirely)

**The 3-of-5 rule:** if 3 or more of your 5 interviews surface red flags, the B2B2C channel needs to be rebuilt or you go pure B2C. Don't dismiss this signal even if it's inconvenient.

## Synthesis (after all 5)

In one sitting, write a 1-page memo with:

- The single most common pain point across all 5 (this is your IBCLC value prop)
- The single most common objection to recommending Latched (this is your channel risk)
- The most common existing recommendation behavior (Huckleberry? Kelly Mom? Pamphlets?)
- The hospital policy variance you observed (was it uniform or wildly different?)
- One quote per interviewee that summarizes their view
- A binary decision: GO / RETHINK on the B2B2C channel

Share that memo with me and we'll update the PRD's Tension 5 with what you learned.
